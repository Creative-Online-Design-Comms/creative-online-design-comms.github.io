What is a Food Forest?

A food forest is a designed ecosystem that mimics the structure and processes of a natural forest, but instead of focusing solely on wild vegetation, it is planted with species that provide food, medicine, fuel, and other human uses. The idea is to layer seven or more types of plants — from tall canopy trees to groundcovers, vines, and root crops — so that every ecological niche is filled. In Otago, where frosts can be severe, droughts frequent, and winds harsh, food forests offer a resilient model for producing food while strengthening local biodiversity. They capture water, improve soil fertility, and buffer against climate extremes, making them a valuable strategy for both urban backyards and rural landscapes.

Food Forest Design

A food forest is carefully structured to mirror the diversity of a woodland, where every plant plays multiple roles. Tall trees provide shade, wind protection, and nuts or fruit; smaller trees and shrubs bear berries and stonefruit; vines climb upwards to harvest sunlight; and roots and herbs create food and medicine while improving soil health. In Otago, the design of such systems is particularly important due to variable conditions ranging from coastal humidity to dry inland summers. By combining deep-rooted perennials with nitrogen fixers, pollinator plants, and groundcovers, these forests conserve water and maintain fertility with minimal external inputs. When designed well, they become living pantries and pharmacies, sustaining families while supporting wildlife and strengthening communities over generations.

Otago Biomes

Alpine Foothills

Cold nights, short summers, frost pockets, stony soils.

Alpine Foothills

In the alpine foothills of Otago, food forests must adapt to challenging microclimates where cold nights, late frosts, and rocky soils are common. Designing here begins with mapping the way cold air drains across slopes and valleys, since frost pockets can devastate tender blossoms in spring.

Stones and dark mulches are used as passive solar batteries, capturing heat during the day and releasing it at night to protect crops. Hardy fruits such as quince, plum, and apricot are chosen for their ability to withstand temperature extremes, and they are paired with frost-tolerant herbs, flowers, and nitrogen-fixing groundcovers.

Guilds of sage, thyme, clover, and comfrey both stabilize the soil and provide insectary functions, encouraging pollinators and beneficial predators. By creatively using natural patterns and working with the flows of air and sun, growers can transform this harsh biome into one of resilience, where abundance grows out of challenge.

Alpine Foothills

🌲 Canopy

Quince, heritage apples (e.g., Sturmer, Monty’s Surprise, Ballarat), chestnut, hardy pear (Winter Nelis, Buerre Bosc), hazelnut on sheltered sites, black walnut (cautious planting due to allelopathy), elder (as both canopy and shelter), hardy crabapples, and service trees (Sorbus domestica).

🌳 Understory

Plum (European and Japanese), apricot (in sheltered, sunny pockets), feijoa (cold-hardy cultivars), medlar, mulberry (dwarf hardy types), peach and nectarine (best against stone or brick heat sinks), cornelian cherry, small cider pears, Siberian pea shrub (Caragana arborescens as a nitrogen-fixer and windbreak understory).

🌿 Shrub

Currants (black, red, white, pink), gooseberries, jostaberry, blueberry (if soil acidity is managed), lingonberry, chokeberry (Aronia), honeyberry (Lonicera caerulea), elderberry shrubs, hazel suckers forming shrub thickets, viburnums (edible species), and hardy rugosa rose (for hips and shelter).

🌱 Herb

Comfrey (bocking cultivars), yarrow, thyme (creeping and common), oregano, sage, calendula, borage, fennel (for pollinators, but can be invasive), anise hyssop, tansy, wormwood, chamomile, lemon balm, lovage, angelica, dill, coriander, parsley, and alpine strawberries as semi-herbaceous ground herbs.

🍓 Groundcover

Strawberries (alpine and garden), white and red clover, creeping thyme, nasturtium, ajuga (bugleweed), violets, wintergreen, sweet woodruff, chickweed, miner’s lettuce, and alpine sorrel (Rumex scutatus). Groundcovers here should protect from frost-heave and stabilise thin soils.

🌾 Climbers

Hops (Humulus lupulus), hardy grapes (Concord, Isabella, American hybrids), scarlet runner bean (perennial root), climbing peas, and kiwiberry (Actinidia arguta — only in sheltered alpine valleys with reduced frost risk).

🥕 Root

Jerusalem artichoke, carrot, beetroot, parsnip, swede, turnip, daikon radish, horseradish, scorzonera, salsify, chicory root, skirret, garlic, onion, shallot, Welsh onion, perennial leeks, and kūmara (in only the warmest alpine pockets, often unsuccessful without heat sinks).

🍄 Fungi

Oyster mushroom logs, shiitake logs, wine cap mushrooms (Stropharia) in mulch, morel inoculation in woodchip beds, mycorrhizal inoculants for apple and nut trees, and native fungi associated with beech or manuka for soil health. Fungi are especially valuable here for nutrient cycling in lean stony soils.

Guild Suggestions (x4)

Warm Pocket Guild: Quince + sage + creeping thyme + comfrey. Works best near rocks or walls that hold heat, balancing fruit production with hardy perennials.

Berry Shelter Guild: Plum + gooseberry + clover + calendula. Creates a low-maintenance, pollinator-friendly guild that tolerates frosts while producing multiple food yields.

Pollinator Strip Guild: Apricot + yarrow + borage + honeyberry. Enhances spring pollination and soil health, making it ideal for short alpine summers with late frosts.

Soil Builder Guild: Pear + Siberian pea shrub + alfalfa + violets. A system focused on nitrogen fixation, groundcover resilience, and long-term soil fertility improvement.

Handy Tips

  • Use stone walls and rock piles as passive heat sinks — they radiate warmth through the night, protecting blossoms.
  • Plant hedges or shelterbelts across frost flows; even a 1–2 °C lift can save entire harvests.
  • Lay frost cloth over low shrubs in spring — this extends the fruiting season for tender plants like apricot.
  • Group early bloomers like quince with later bloomers like plum to stagger flowering and reduce total frost loss.

Fun Fact: The Lindis Pass can drop below –10 °C in winter, yet blackcurrants and gooseberries still thrive, proving their alpine toughness.

Central Otago Drylands

Hot summers, frosty winters, low rainfall, schist soils.

Central Otago Drylands

Central Otago is renowned for its hot, dry summers, frosty winters, and lean schist-derived soils, making it one of the toughest but also most rewarding biomes for food forest design. The starting point is water — capturing every drop through swales, basins, and mulched hollows to combat rainfall scarcity.

Species are carefully selected to endure both drought and frost, with heritage apples, almonds, and stonefruit thriving when combined with deep-rooted comfrey, drought-hardy thyme and lavender, and clover for nitrogen fixation. Seasonal rhythms guide management: spring grafting of apple cultivars, summer irrigation at depth, autumn planting of perennials, and winter pruning to maintain form and airflow.

Thermal mass such as schist rocks or gravel mulches helps buffer the extremes of heat and cold, creating stable microclimates for plants. With thoughtful design, these drylands can support productive, long-lived agroecosystems that embody the principles of storing energy, valuing diversity, and designing for resilience in a climate of extremes.

🌲 Canopy

Walnut, black walnut (care with allelopathy), chestnut, heritage apples (Granny Smith, Sturmer, Cox’s Orange Pippin), cider apples, hardy pears (Winter Nelis, Nashi, Beurré Bosc), almond, hardy apricot, mulberry, service tree, and ginkgo. These thrive best on north-facing slopes with water-harvesting earthworks.

🌳 Understory

Quince, medlar, feijoa (hardy cultivars), peach and nectarine against stone walls, cornelian cherry, smaller apple cultivars, olive in warm microsites, Siberian pea shrub, and hawthorn. Understory species add fruit resilience and soil stability.

🌿 Shrub

Currants, gooseberries, jostaberry, blueberries (acid soils), elderberry, Aronia, seaberry (Hippophae), lavender, rosemary, sage shrubs, Cistus (rock rose), broom (nitrogen fixer), and rugosa rose for hips. Shrubs provide shelter and pollinator support.

🌱 Herb

Comfrey, thyme, oregano, sage, yarrow, tansy, fennel, dill, coriander, chamomile, calendula, marjoram, borage, lemon balm, wormwood, lovage, anise hyssop, hyssop, savory, sorrel, horseradish, and echinacea. Herbs conserve soil moisture and attract beneficial insects.

🍓 Groundcover

Creeping thyme, clover (red, white, subterranean), nasturtium, purslane, alpine strawberry, ajuga, chickweed, creeping comfrey, mint (controlled), dandelion, miner’s lettuce, and buckwheat as cover crop. These reduce evaporation and protect soils.

🌾 Climbers

Grapes (Pinot Noir, Riesling, Isabella), beans (runner, French), climbing peas, hops (less vigorous in dryland), and kiwiberry in watered niches. Climbers add vertical yield and shade.

🥕 Root

Garlic, onion, shallot, leek, parsnip, carrot, beetroot, turnip, swede, daikon radish, scorzonera, chicory root, skirret, horseradish, Jerusalem artichoke, saffron crocus, and globe artichoke. Roots ensure long-term food storage in harsh conditions.

🍄 Fungi

Wine cap mushrooms in mulch, oyster mushrooms, shiitake logs, and mycorrhizal fungi linked to nuts and apples. Fungi are vital for water retention and soil structure in schist soils.

Guild Suggestions (x4)

Apple Guild: Apple + comfrey + thyme + clover + yarrow. A resilient mix balancing fruit production, pollinator habitat, and soil fertility in drylands.

Almond Guild: Almond + lavender + rosemary + clover. Combines a nut crop with drought-hardy aromatic herbs and nitrogen fixers, excellent for bees.

Stonefruit Guild: Apricot + sage + thyme + seaberry. Designed for slope planting, this guild resists wind, attracts pollinators, and improves nitrogen cycling.

Shelter Guild: Pear + Siberian pea shrub + rugosa rose + fennel. Provides multi-layer shelter, insectary value, and edible yields, stabilising soil near swales.

Handy Tips

  • Mulching cuts irrigation needs by half — vital in dry summers.
  • Use swales and basins to catch every drop of rain and snowmelt.
  • Plant lavender and thyme strips as living mulch that also attract bees.
  • Place heat-retaining stones near apricots and peaches to extend ripening seasons.

Fun Fact: The thyme fields near Alexandra bloom so intensely they can be seen from space in summer!

Coastal Otago

Wind, salt spray, mild winters, sandy/loamy soils.

Coastal Otago

Coastal Otago presents unique challenges and opportunities for food forest design, shaped by persistent winds, salt-laden air, and sandy soils. Establishing effective shelter is the first priority, with living windbreaks of salt-tolerant natives such as harakeke, coprosma, and pittosporum creating calmer microclimates.

Once shelter is secured, soils are improved with composted seaweed and organic matter, building fertility and water retention while recycling local resources. Planting emphasises species that thrive in salt air, including feijoa, citrus in warm niches, and hardy Mediterranean herbs like rosemary, thyme, and oregano. Layered guilds integrate groundcovers such as nasturtium and clover to reduce erosion, attract pollinators, and supply nitrogen.

Prunings from shelterbelts are recycled as mulch, closing the loop on waste. This coastal food forest model not only yields fruit, herbs, and perennial vegetables but also strengthens dunes and coastal soils, offering both ecological protection and human abundance along Otago’s windswept shores.

🌲 Canopy

Salt-tolerant apple cultivars, feijoa trained as small canopy trees, macrocarpa for shelterbelts, pohutukawa (northern coastal ornament with nectar), Norfolk pine, and hardy pears in inland pockets. Canopy species double as shelter and food producers near the coast.

🌳 Understory

Feijoa, citrus (lemon, mandarin, lime) in warm niches, loquat, guava (cherry guava/Psidium cattleianum), persimmon in protected bays, elder, and hardy olives. Understory thrives with airflow pruning to reduce fungal risk.

🌿 Shrub

Rosemary, coprosma (edible berries and shelter), blueberry (acidic sand pockets), Chilean guava, hydrangea as shelter, lavender, saltbush (Atriplex spp.), and rugosa rose for hips. Shrubs here stabilise dunes and resist salt spray.

🌱 Herb

Thyme, oregano, parsley, fennel, dill, borage, mint (controlled patches), calendula, chamomile, yarrow, comfrey, and nasturtium. Herbs are resilient to salt winds, attract pollinators, and provide culinary and medicinal harvests.

🍓 Groundcover

Clover, nasturtium, creeping thyme, New Zealand spinach (Tetragonia tetragonioides), violets, purslane, miner’s lettuce, and strawberries. Groundcovers prevent erosion, fix nitrogen, and provide soil cover against wind and salt stress.

🌾 Climbers

Grapes (sheltered coastal hillsides), hardy kiwiberry, beans, peas, and passionfruit (in the warmest microsites). Climbers make use of wind-protected niches while adding vertical productivity.

🥕 Root

Carrot, beetroot, parsnip, radish, turnip, kūmara (in sandy soils with warmth), yacon, Jerusalem artichoke, garlic, and onion. Root crops adapt well to sandy coastal soils with organic matter addition.

🍄 Fungi

Wine cap mushrooms in seaweed-rich mulch, oyster mushrooms on logs, shiitake in shaded pockets, and beneficial mycorrhizal fungi tied to coastal natives like harakeke and pohutukawa. Fungi cycle nutrients and improve sandy soil structure.

Guild Suggestions (x4)

Feijoa Guild: Feijoa + rosemary + nasturtium + clover. A resilient coastal guild that stabilises soil, supports pollinators, and yields fruit and herbs.

Citrus Guild: Lemon + thyme + parsley + calendula. A warm-niche guild combining citrus with herbs that deter pests and provide culinary harvests.

Salt-Edge Guild: Olive + lavender + oregano + New Zealand spinach. A guild for sandy, exposed edges, blending drought-hardy herbs with productive crops.

Shelter Guild: Coprosma + rugosa rose + clover + fennel. Functions as a living shelterbelt, while providing hips, berries, nitrogen, and insect habitat.

Handy Tips

  • Salt spray is filtered more effectively by porous, living shelterbelts than by solid walls.
  • Apply seaweed mulch regularly to restore minerals and improve sandy soils.
  • Use drip irrigation to reduce evaporation losses in coastal winds.
  • Prune citrus to improve airflow and reduce fungal issues in humid pockets.

Fun Fact: The Otago Peninsula is home to New Zealand’s only mainland royal albatross colony, thriving in the same winds that shape coastal gardens.

River Valleys

Fertile alluvial soils, risk of waterlogging, broad flats.

River Valleys

Otago’s river valleys are rich in alluvial soils deposited over centuries, providing some of the most fertile growing conditions in the region. However, these low-lying flats are vulnerable to waterlogging during floods or heavy rain, so careful hydrological design is key.

Food forests in this biome often begin with berms or raised beds to lift sensitive roots above saturated ground, while swales and riparian plantings help manage water flow and prevent erosion. The fertility of these soils makes them ideal for nut trees such as walnuts and hazelnuts, which in turn create dappled shade for berries like currants, gooseberries, and raspberries.

Companion planting with nitrogen fixers, insect-attracting herbs, and low groundcovers maintains soil structure and fertility while supporting biodiversity. Guided by principles of small and slow solutions and integration, these river valley forests can become resilient, multi-layered systems that combine high productivity with flood resilience and ecological stewardship.

🌲 Canopy

Walnuts, hazelnuts, alder (nitrogen-fixer), poplar (for timber and windbreak), and tall pears. These thrive in fertile alluvial soils with good drainage design.

🌳 Understory

Pears, plums, apples, feijoa, and quince planted on berms. They make full use of rich soils while avoiding root waterlogging.

🌿 Shrub

Currants, gooseberries, raspberries, elderberry, Aronia, and rugosa rose. Shrubs thrive in dapple shade beneath nut canopies.

🌱 Herb

Chives, comfrey, yarrow, mint, parsley, calendula, and dill. Herbs recycle nutrients, repel pests, and support pollinators.

🍓 Groundcover

Strawberries, clover, violets, ajuga, miner’s lettuce, and sweet woodruff. These stabilise moist soils and suppress weeds.

🌾 Climbers

Beans, peas, grapes, and hops. Climbers use canopy support and benefit from fertile conditions.

🥕 Root

Garlic, leek, onion, kūmara (on mounds), carrot, beetroot, parsnip, and daikon radish. Raised beds improve drainage for these crops.

🍄 Fungi

Oyster mushrooms, shiitake logs, and wine caps in mulch. Beneficial fungi enhance fertility and flood resilience.

Guild Suggestions (x4)

Nut Guild: Walnut + blackcurrants + chives + clover + violets. Shade-tolerant guild that thrives beneath large canopy trees while improving soil fertility.

Berry Guild: Hazelnut + raspberries + comfrey + yarrow. Combines nuts with berry yields and beneficial insect attractors in fertile soils.

Riparian Guild: Alder + elderberry + mint + clover. A flood-resilient planting along waterways that stabilises banks and cycles nutrients.

Fruit Guild: Pear + gooseberries + calendula + strawberries. A productive guild blending tree, shrub, herb, and groundcover layers in rich flats.

Handy Tips

  • Always raise root crops above flood level using berms or raised beds.
  • Riparian strips of native plants filter runoff and reduce erosion.
  • Dappled shade under nuts reduces stress on shallow-rooted berries.
  • Comfrey and clover are excellent for soil structure in wet conditions.

Fun Fact: The Clutha River carries enough silt each year to build new islands.

Dunedin Lowlands

Sheltered bays, rich soils, moderate frosts, humid at times.

Dunedin Lowlands

The lowland areas around Dunedin are among the most favourable environments for food forest establishment, offering deep soils, shelter from extreme weather, and moderate frosts. However, their high humidity requires careful attention to airflow and fungal control.

This is addressed through thoughtful pruning, layered planting for ventilation, and guilds that include aromatic herbs with antifungal properties. Apples remain a cornerstone crop, thriving in the temperate conditions, and are often paired with strawberries and currants beneath them to make full use of space and create weed-suppressing groundcovers.

Comfrey, clover, and chives strengthen soil fertility while attracting pollinators. Hedges and shelterbelts, often incorporating native species, not only provide wind protection but also act as corridors for birds and beneficial insects. Designed with self-regulation and yield in mind, Dunedin’s lowland food forests offer a balanced system that supports families, builds biodiversity, and reconnects communities with their rich horticultural heritage.

🌲 Canopy

Apples, pears, feijoa trained as standards, chestnut in larger lowlands, and elder. These thrive in deep soils with shelter from wind.

🌳 Understory

Plums, feijoa, elder, medlar, peach, and nectarine in sheltered pockets. Understory trees fill gaps beneath canopy orchards.

🌿 Shrub

Currants, gooseberries, blueberries, raspberries, and Chilean guava. Shrubs add resilience and diversity under humid conditions.

🌱 Herb

Comfrey, parsley, chives, mint, oregano, sage, coriander, calendula, and yarrow. Aromatic herbs help suppress fungal issues.

🍓 Groundcover

Strawberries, clover, violets, creeping thyme, ajuga, and nasturtium. These knit soils, reduce weeds, and create multi-yield layers.

🌾 Climbers

Beans, peas, hops, and hardy kiwiberry. Climbers add vertical yield in sheltered niches.

🥕 Root

Carrots, beetroot, parsnip, kūmara in warm soils, onions, garlic, and perennial leeks. Root crops benefit from mulch against humidity swings.

🍄 Fungi

Wine caps, oyster mushrooms, shiitake logs, and mycorrhizal fungi supporting orchard health. Fungi recycle organic matter and suppress soil-borne disease.

Guild Suggestions (x4)

Apple Guild: Apple + strawberries + comfrey + chives + clover. A classic lowland guild that supports yield, fertility, and fungal resistance.

Berry Guild: Elder + raspberries + mint + calendula. Combines shrub fruit with aromatic herbs that manage humidity and attract insects.

Nut Guild: Chestnut + currants + oregano + clover. Creates a productive food and forage guild with both fruit and nut yields.

Pollinator Guild: Feijoa + gooseberries + yarrow + nasturtium. A pollinator-rich guild balancing soil cover, insect habitat, and food yields.

Handy Tips

  • Prune for airflow to reduce fungal risk in humid conditions.
  • Mulch strawberries beneath apples to reduce weeds and retain soil moisture.
  • Use hedgerows with native species as wind buffers and wildlife corridors.
  • Choose heritage apple varieties for disease resistance and local adaptation.

Fun Fact: Dunedin once hosted orchards with over 150 unique apple varieties, many still found in backyards today.

Seasonal Calendar

🌱 Spring

Spring in Otago is a time of rapid growth and careful timing, when frosts can still strike unexpectedly even as warmth returns.

This is the best season to plant berry canes such as raspberries and gooseberries, graft apples and pears onto hardy rootstocks, and sow fast-growing greens to fill early gaps.

Protecting vulnerable blossoms with frost cloth or by planting in sheltered niches is critical for success.

Nitrogen-fixing legumes like peas and clover are sown to enrich the soil, while herbs and insect-attracting flowers are introduced to support pollinators.

Mulching basins around young trees retains moisture and buffers temperature swings, helping seedlings establish strong roots.

Spring sets the tone for the year ahead, requiring observation, patience, and resilience as plants awaken and ecosystems re-balance after winter’s dormancy.

☀️ Summer

Summer in Otago brings long daylight hours, heat, and in inland areas, intense dryness that tests both plants and growers.

This is the season of abundant harvests, with strawberries, currants, and stonefruit ripening, providing the reward for careful planning.

Irrigation becomes a central task, best applied deeply and infrequently to encourage strong root systems rather than shallow surface growth.

Mulching is vital, keeping soils cool and moist, while also suppressing weeds that compete for limited resources.

Herbs such as basil, thyme, and rosemary thrive in the warmth, adding both flavour and biodiversity to guilds.

Summer is also the time for pruning vigorous growth, guiding airflow to reduce fungal risks, and shaping trees for balanced form.

With careful stewardship, the season’s abundance can be captured and stored, feeding households long after the summer sun has set.

🍂 Autumn

Autumn is the season of grounding and preparation, when the land cools but retains enough warmth to encourage deep root establishment.

This is the best time to plant long-lived fruit and nut trees, ensuring they settle into soil before winter dormancy.

Cover crops such as clover and rye are sown to knit bare ground, fixing nitrogen while preventing erosion from seasonal rains.

Fallen leaves and garden prunings are gathered into compost, closing nutrient loops and building organic matter for future growth.

Autumn is also the season of preserving — apples, plums, and berries are harvested, stored, and transformed into preserves, ensuring food security through the winter.

Planning hedges, windbreaks, and shelter belts at this time helps strengthen the site’s resilience before storms arrive.

Guided by the principle of catching and storing energy, autumn work lays the foundation for enduring fertility and abundance.

❄️ Winter

Winter in Otago is both a time of rest and strategic activity, when the landscape slows under frost, snow, and cold rain.

Trees and shrubs retreat into dormancy, offering the perfect opportunity for pruning to shape structure, improve airflow, and remove diseased branches.

Adding compost and organic matter at this stage allows soil organisms to break it down in time for spring planting.

Roots are protected with mulch, straw, or even living groundcovers that insulate against freezing temperatures.

Winter is also the season for planning — mapping frost lines, observing drainage patterns, and redesigning guilds based on the year’s successes and failures.

In sheltered pockets, hardy greens like kale and winter brassicas continue to provide a steady yield, showing how resilience carries through even the harshest conditions.

By working slowly and deliberately, winter becomes a season of renewal, setting the stage for the burst of life that follows in spring.
